About This Website

Introduction

I started this website during the late oughts as a more personal outlet separate from my professional, academic homepage. The name Apparebit is the third-person singular future active indicative of the Latin verb appareo and translates to “it will appear.” The choice of name is programmatic: The website started as a showcase for my photography. Its mission has since expanded to include my writing as well. Apparebit has seen two major redesigns, covering information architecture, content, and appearance—with each redesign also being far more ambitious than the previous version.

History

  1. The first version of Apparebit featured a rotating cast of photographs under a couple of headings and was rather simplistic—falling short on information architecture, semantic markup, accessibility, and responsiveness. It also was an exercise in brutalist design long before brutalist design became fashionable. Quite possibly, that was a late effect of forays into graphic design during my late teens. At the time, I was experimenting with found images and found type, creating printed matter as well as t-shirts. Those efforts culminated in my first conscious use of a typeface, Akzidenz-Grotesk Buch, for a series of posters promoting gay dance parties in my hometown Würzburg. With my education demanding ever more of my attention, my design exploits went on hiatus shortly thereafter. But an affinity for (neo-)grotesque type and clean sans serif forms persisted until fairly recently.
  2. The second version, released in April 2012, was rather serious, presenting my photography as art. I did indeed participate in a couple of group shows and also won an award for my work at the time. Where the first version fell short on best practices, the second version made up for it and then some. It was semantic, accessible, and responsive—seamlessly scaling from smartphone screens to desktop monitors. It religiously adhered to a vertical rhythm, went out of its way to support Internet Explorer, and could even be installed as a smartphone app. Alas, its design also was bland and utilitarian. Worse, it suffered from an overly generous application of shadows, gradients, and highlights. I removed those imitations of light in August 2015. Thereafter, Apparebit’s design was flat, bland, and utilitarian.
  3. The need for a third version became apparent a couple of years ago. I was using Facebook as a de facto blog, commenting on art, technology, and politics—plus the occasional cat pictures. I had also published a first long-form article on this website. Seeking to make Apparebit my primary outlet, I set out for a new design as well as new software. I expanded my knowledge about graphics and typography, conducted countless visual experiments and studies, and explored different approaches to software tooling. While very instructive, the scope of these efforts was too broad and thus got in the way of actually producing a result. While writing and designing my article about Facebook, I realized that what I had originally conceived as an independent project really was putting the finishing touches on my full website redesign. That doesn’t mean the redesign is complete. Notably, my photography still is missing. But I have a robust and appealing foundation for presenting my work.

Typography

Apparebit’s design features typefaces by contemporary designers from Argentina, France, and the United States. Body text throughout this website is set in Bely by Roxane Gataud, which is available through TypeTogether. Headings and pullquotes vary between pages and, in addition to Bely Display, use the following typefaces:

  • Bild designed by David Jonathan Ross and released through his Font of the Month Club;
  • Bluu Suuperstar designed by Jean-Baptiste Morizot and released through Black Foundry;
  • Reforma 2018 designed by Alejandro Lo Celso and released under the Creative Commons BY-ND 4.0 license.

Finally, the website logo and icon use the stenciled version of Super Fat Bob also designed by Jean-Baptiste Morizot and released through FutureFonts.

FutureFonts and Ross’ Font of the Month Club are great resources for new type designs. Their typefaces may not come in as many variants as those in more conventional outlets and they may also be very much in-progress. But they are always interesting and fresh. They also tend to be affordable, while providing some measure of a steady income to independent type designers. A win-win for all parties involved!
